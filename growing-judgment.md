# Growing judgment

If [judgment is the job](principles.md) and agents have eaten the reps judgment used to grow from, then developing people is no longer incidental to running an agent function — it's the constraint on the whole thing. This is how the function grows taste on purpose.

---

## The supply problem

Taste was never taught. It accumulated as the residue of doing junior work — badly, repeatedly, with real consequences — until you'd internalized what "good" felt like and could tell when something was subtly off. The reps *were* the curriculum.

Agents now do those reps. The junior no longer builds the list by hand, writes the bad first draft, or runs the enrichment that teaches them why the data is dirty. So the substrate judgment grows from is disappearing, right as judgment becomes the scarce, decisive skill. This is a **supply-of-judgment problem**, and it doesn't solve itself — faster agentic iteration makes *more output*, not more taste, because procedural skill is encoded by effortful, self-generated repetition over time, not by throughput.

If you don't rebuild the reps deliberately, you get a team that can operate agents and cannot tell when the agent is wrong. That's the failure mode this whole section exists to prevent.

→ *[The Taste Famine — transferring judgment when agents have eaten the reps](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/how-transfer-judgment-when-agents-have-eaten-reps-kai-karlstrom-2vyoe/)*

## The transfer architecture

Five practices that rebuild the substrate on purpose.

**1. Predict, then compare.** Before a junior sees the agent's output, they predict what good output should be. Then they compare. The gap between their prediction and the result — and between the result and *actually* good — is where the rep lives. Consuming the agent's output without first committing to a prediction teaches nothing; it just outsources the judgment.

**2. Narrate the rejected options.** Most taste is tacit — we know more than we can tell. The clean explanation of the choice you made is the least useful part; the transferable part is the **options you rejected and why they were subtly wrong.** Show your reasoning as a fork, not a conclusion.

**3. Show your own work with AI.** Narrate your own prompt failures, your own wrong turns, the confident answer you didn't trust and why. Apprenticeship has migrated from subject-matter expertise to **meta-expertise — how you work with the agent** — and that's learned by watching someone do it in the open, mistakes included.

**4. Move taste through proximity, not documentation.** Tacit judgment doesn't survive being written into a doc; the reader has no experiential base for it to land on. It transfers by working *next to* someone on real cases. Documentation captures the rails and the rules; proximity captures the taste. You need both, and you can't substitute one for the other.

**5. Keep more than one target in rotation.** Don't clone the star. If everyone calibrates to one person's taste, you get a monoculture that inherits that person's blind spots and collapses when they leave. Expose people to several strong judgment models so they synthesize their own.

## Onboarding a new hire into an agent function

The first thing a new hire needs isn't the tool list — it's a **map of the context the agents run against**, and the standards they'll be judged by. The fast path into an unfamiliar domain is to structure the context deliberately so both you and the agents have something solid to work from, rather than trying to hold it all in your head. (This is the "context OS" move — building the structured ground truth first, then working on top of it.)

A concrete first month:

1. **Read the [principles](principles.md) and the [thinking](the-thinking.md).** Understand the *why* before touching a run. The model is upstream of the mechanics.
2. **Predict-then-compare on real runs, under a reviewer.** Bounded fleet, low stakes, every result checked against a prediction they committed to first.
3. **Own a slice, with the rails owned by someone else.** Real work, real consequences, but the guardrails and the outcome accountability still sit with an experienced owner.
4. **Earn the autonomy ratchet.** Fleet size and decision rights expand on demonstrated judgment — not on tenure, and not by default ([decision rights](operating-the-team.md)).

The goal of onboarding isn't to make someone fast at running agents. Agents are already fast. It's to make them **someone who can tell when the fast, confident output is wrong** — because that's the only thing the function actually needs from a human.
