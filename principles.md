# Operating principles

Eight beliefs this function runs on. They aren't aspirations on a wall — each one changes a concrete decision about how the team is built and how the work is reviewed. Each links to the essay that argues it in full.

---

### 1. Lead a layer up

Leadership leverage is not in running the agents, and it is not in doing the work yourself. It's a layer up — in **setting the intent and the playbook the agents run on**. Pick that layer and stay at it. A leader who drifts down into operating the agents has become an expensive operator; a leader who drifts down into doing the work has stopped leading the function and started competing with it.

*In practice:* the leader's output is the playbook, the standards, and the intent — not the runs. Time spent tuning an individual agent's prompt is time the layer above went unmanaged.

→ *[The Layer Question — where leaders belong when teams run AI](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/layer-question-where-leaders-belong-when-teams-run-ai-kai-karlstrom-qnzae)*

---

### 2. Provision protection, not expertise

For fifty years a leader provisioned **expertise** — they knew more, the team learned from them. AI commoditized that. The strand a leader provisions now is **protection**: shielding people from being *blamed* when an agent fails, *replaced* by the system they themselves build, and *embarrassed* by their own agent's confident-but-wrong output. Absorb blame upward and route it correctly. Make each person's agent their leverage, not their threat.

*In practice:* the leader wears the failure in public. Fleet size is capped at what a human can still hold ground truth on. Kill authority is pre-authorized, not requested mid-incident.

→ *[The Expertise Need Is Dissolving. Protection Is What Leaders Provision Now.](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/expertise-need-dissolving-protection-what-leaders-now-kai-karlstrom-ea5ae/)*

---

### 3. Be the friction

When every agent hands you a clean, confident answer, the highest-value thing a leader can be is the **one source that doesn't just confirm you** — the friction that asks again precisely when the answer came back too clean. The org chart that follows reshapes toward small human teams that can push back, not swarms of agents that can't.

*In practice:* review exists to disconfirm, not to rubber-stamp. A review that never sends anything back isn't a review. The clean answer gets *more* scrutiny, not less.

→ *[Leadership in the Age of AI Agents (The Leader as Friction)](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/leadership-age-ai-agents-kai-karlstrom-umdfe)*

---

### 4. Judgment is the job

Once execution is commoditized, the durable skill is **taste** — knowing when the clean, confident answer is wrong. It's the one leadership skill that survives the agent, because it's the one thing the agent is structurally worst at: evaluating its own output against a standard it can't feel.

*In practice:* hire and promote for judgment, not throughput. The person who can tell you *why* the polished draft is subtly wrong is worth more than the one who produced it faster.

→ *[Developing Taste — the only leadership skill that survives the agent](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/developing-taste-only-leadership-skill-survives-agent-kai-karlstrom-zywxe)*

---

### 5. Make doubt sayable

Most agent failures are **silent** — the run stays green while the work drifts wrong. The cheapest place to catch that is a human who can say *"this looks wrong, I can't say why yet"* and stop the run, before the drift compounds into a red metric. That only happens if you've made inarticulate doubt safe to voice. Psychological safety (Edmondson) isn't a soft nicety here; it's the primary detector for a whole class of failure your dashboards miss.

*In practice:* anyone can halt a run on an unarticulated hunch, without having to justify it first. You **fund the false alarm** — the person who stops a run that turns out fine is thanked, not corrected.

→ *[Permission for Inarticulate Doubt](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/permission-inarticulate-doubt-kai-karlstrom-5glue)*

---

### 6. Hand off the build

The build a leader can do *fastest* is exactly the one they should hand off. Doing it yourself blinds you to the execution layer you've left — and the team's hands on that build are your only real view of how it behaves. "I'm faster" is the wrong reason to keep it; speed at the build buys blindness. Build to *set the pattern and show your work*, then hand it off — and dip back into the execution layer deliberately, to stay calibrated.

*In practice:* the leader builds the first one as a reference, then gives it away. Hoarding it because of the IKEA effect or the competency trap costs you the view.

→ *[The Builder's Trap — why the faster leader should give the build away](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/builders-trap-why-leaders-should-delegate-age-ai-kai-karlstrom-opsje/)*

---

### 7. Rebuild the reps on purpose

Taste was never taught — it accumulated as the residue of doing junior work badly, repeatedly, with real consequences. Agents now do those reps, so the substrate judgment grows from is disappearing. This is a **supply-of-judgment problem**, and faster agentic iteration does not solve it: procedural skill is encoded by effortful, self-generated repetition, not by throughput. So you rebuild the reps deliberately — predict-then-compare, narrate the rejected options, move taste through proximity, not documentation.

*In practice:* juniors predict what the agent should produce *before* they see it, then compare. The leader narrates the options they rejected, not just the one they chose. Taste transfers by working next to someone, not by reading a doc.

→ *[The Taste Famine — transferring judgment when agents have eaten the reps](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/how-transfer-judgment-when-agents-have-eaten-reps-kai-karlstrom-2vyoe/)*

---

### 8. A named human owns the outcome

When an agent can initiate a workflow, "who owns the outcome?" cannot be answered after the fact — you can't reconstruct a decision no human made once it's burned you. So a **named human owns the outcome up front**: they own the rails the agent runs on, they review the escalations, and they sample the rest. This is *human-owns-the-rails*, deliberately not *human-eyeballs-every-action* — the latter is the fig leaf that doesn't scale and doesn't work. Accountability is a design property of the function, not a report bolted on after.

*In practice:* every agent workflow has one named owner before it runs. They're accountable for outcomes they didn't individually produce — so they get real authority over the rails, and the mandate to sample, not just to sign off.

→ *This principle is drawn from work-in-progress on production agent governance; it's the management stance, held separately from the private architecture that implements it.*

---

These eight are consistent because they share one root: **deterministic accountability wrapped around probabilistic work.** The machine is probabilistic and always will be. The leadership around it — who owns what, what gets stopped, who is protected, how judgment is grown — is the deterministic layer that makes the probabilistic layer safe to run. That's the whole model in one line.
